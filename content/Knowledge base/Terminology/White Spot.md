>[!info] **What it is**
>
A _white spot_ is a small, bright, usually circular area that’s noticeably lighter than the surrounding pixels when the screen is on. It doesn’t go away regardless of the image being displayed, unlike temporary image artifacts.

### **Main causes**

>[!note]
>White spots in LCDs typically come from **physical or mechanical issues** rather than purely electrical ones:

1. **Pressure marks from behind the panel**
   
	- The LCD stack has several layers — polarizers, liquid crystal layer, color filters, and a backlight diffuser.
	- If something presses unevenly on the backlight diffuser (for example, a speck of dust, a manufacturing misalignment, or a warped frame), it can create a localized bright area.
	- Common in laptops where screws or stand-offs press too hard on the back of the panel.

2. **Damaged diffuser sheet in the backlight assembly**

	- The diffuser spreads the LED light evenly.
	- If it gets dented or scratched, light concentrates in one spot, making it look brighter.

3. **Defective or misaligned prism sheet / light guide plate**

	- These layers channel light evenly across the display.
	- Misalignment or a defect can let extra light “escape” at one location.

4. **Air gaps or contamination in the backlight layers**

	- Even a tiny foreign particle between layers can scatter light, causing a white “hotspot.”

### **How it’s different from other defects**

- **Dead/stuck pixels** → very small, single-pixel dots, caused by transistor issues in the TFT array.

- **Backlight bleed** → light leakage along screen edges, not isolated spots.

- **White spot defect** → larger (often 2–8 mm), fuzzy-edged bright area caused by optical layer irregularities.

### **Repair / mitigation**

- In consumer devices, this often requires replacing the **entire LCD module**, since the backlight stack isn’t serviceable without cleanroom conditions.

- If caused by frame pressure, loosening nearby screws or slightly adjusting the panel’s seating can sometimes reduce it (but risky — can cause more damage).

- In manufacturing, white spot detection is part of QA, since it’s considered a **Grade B/C defect** depending on severity and location.
